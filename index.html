The Village

Nestled in the Ash Valley

Wareside is a small, unspoilt village in East Hertfordshire, set amid rolling farmland in the valley of the River Ash. Approximately four miles from the market town of Ware, it has a population of around 600 dwellings widely dispersed through the parish. Records of habitation stretch back to the Norman Conquest — several local place names derive from French nobility rewarded after 1066. Today the village retains its rural character, with a beautiful Victorian church, two well-loved pubs, a historic manor hotel, and walks through some of Hertfordshire's quietest countryside.

Through the Ages

A History of Wareside

From Domesday to the present day

1066

Norman Conquest

Records of habitation begin at the time of William the Conqueror. Several local place names — including Cold Christmas and Helham Green — derive from French nobility rewarded with lands after 1066.

1699

Fanhams Hall Founded

John Evans purchased the Fanhams estate in 1699 and built the original Hall around 1715 in Queen Anne style, possibly incorporating elements of a 1666 structure.

1841

Holy Trinity Church Built

Architect Thomas Smith designed Holy Trinity Church in the Lombardic style, consecrated in 1841 and listed Grade II in 1967. It remains the spiritual heart of the village today.

1844

Parish of Ware Rural Formed

An ecclesiastical parish was created from surrounding hamlets in the ancient Parish of Thundridge and the rural communities to the north-east of Ware.

1870

Imperial Gazetteer Entry

Wilson's Imperial Gazetteer describes Wareside as "a chapelry in Ware and Thundridge parishes, 2 miles NNE of Ware station" with a population of 696. Grain and gravel were the economic mainstays.

1876

Blakesware Manor Rebuilt

Associated with essayist Charles Lamb, Blakesware Manor was rebuilt by renowned architect George Devey in red-brick Tudor Revival style between 1876 and 1889. The Roman Catholic chapel followed in 1896.

1901

Fanhams Hall Transformed

Mrs Croft commissioned architect W. Wood Bethell to remodel Fanhams Hall in grand Jacobean style. The extraordinary Japanese water garden was laid out 1901–1905 — one of the finest in England.

1991

Parish Renamed Wareside

On 2 May 1991, the Parish of Ware Rural was officially renamed Wareside, taking the name of its largest settlement. The village today remains a thriving rural community governed by its own parish council.

Points of Interest

Wareside's Landmarks

Grade II Listed

Holy Trinity Church

Built in 1841 by Thomas Smith in the distinctive Lombardic style, this Grade II listed church is the village's most prominent landmark. Its Victorian interior is well preserved and it holds Sunday morning services.

Wareside, SG12 7RR Directions
Grade II* Listed

Fanhams Hall

An 18th-century Queen Anne country house rebuilt in grand Jacobean style in 1901. Its 9-hectare grounds include formal English gardens and a remarkable Japanese water garden with authentic stone lanterns and a Japanese house. Now a luxury hotel.

Fanhams Hall Road, SG12 7PZ Directions
Grade II Listed

Blakesware Manor

Rebuilt in red-brick Tudor Revival style by George Devey between 1876 and 1889, with literary associations with essayist Charles Lamb. The adjoining Roman Catholic chapel was added in 1896 by Edward Goldie.

Blakesware, Wareside, SG12 Directions
Village Pub

The Chequers Inn

Dating from the 16th century, originally the Chequers and Punch Bowl. A beloved village local on the B1004, it offers home-cooked food, real fires, and is a favourite starting point for Ash Valley walkers.

B1004, Wareside, SG12 7QY Directions
Now Closed

The White Horse — Closed

One of two survivors from Wareside's seven late-Victorian pubs, the White Horse traded for over 150 years before closing in 2021 and converting to residential use. Its loss is felt by the village — for much of that time it offered a celebrated Greek-Mediterranean menu and warm community atmosphere.

Wareside, SG12 7QX Directions
Walking Country

The Ash Valley & River Ash

The River Ash winds through the parish, giving the village its evocative landscape. The Ash Valley Walk connects Wareside to Ware, Widford, and Hunsdon through meadows and hedgerows largely unchanged for centuries.

On Foot

Local Circular Walks

Four routes through Wareside's fields, riverbanks and ancient woodland — from a gentle riverside ramble to a half-day adventure.
All are circular, starting and finishing at the same point. OS Explorer 194 recommended for all routes.

Walk 1

Blakesware Manor & Buckney Wood

Easy 5 miles · 8 km ~2.5 hours 🐕 Dog Friendly
Start: The Chequers Inn, Wareside, SG12 7QY  ·  OS ref TL 396 156

Cross the River Ash via the old Buntingford Branch line path, then follow quiet green lanes through Upper Crackney to the edge of Blakesware Estate. Cross the Nimney Bourne stream at Baker's End, pass through Buckney Wood with its ancient coppiced ash stools, and return through Reeves Green. Almost entirely flat with no serious hills.

  • Public footpath through Blakesware Estate grounds
  • Follows the course of the old Buntingford Branch railway
  • Ancient multi-stemmed ash coppice in Buckney Wood
  • Nimney Bourne stream and River Ash crossings
  • Finish at The Chequers or White Horse for refreshments

Walk 2

Barrow Hill & the River Ash

Easy / Moderate 4.75 miles · 7.6 km ~2.5 hours 🐕 Dog Friendly
Start: The Chequers Inn, Wareside, SG12 7QY  ·  OS ref TL 396 156

Head north through the dispersed hamlet of Babbs Green, then climb to the summit of Barrow Hill for wide panoramic views across the Ash Valley. Descend to the River Ash and follow the winding riverbank south before returning through open meadows to Wareside. One noticeable climb to Barrow Hill — otherwise gentle, rolling countryside.

  • Panoramic views over the Ash Valley from Barrow Hill
  • Babbs Green hamlet — one of Wareside's oldest settlements
  • Riverside walking along the meandering River Ash
  • Holy Trinity Church at Babbs Green passes en route
  • Can be combined with Walk 1 for an 8.75-mile full-day route

Walk 3

Ware East Loop  (via Wareside)

Moderate 5.7 miles · 9.1 km ~3 hours 🐕 Dog Friendly
Start: East Ware (Bridleway 7 junction), Ware, SG12  ·  w3w: charm.frogs.soils

From the east side of Ware, follow Bridleway 7 through open countryside overlooking the Ash Valley before descending to follow the river through Wareside — crossing the River Ash six times. Stop at The Chequers Inn, trace the old Buntingford Branch railway line and return via Bridleway 11. Some sections can be muddy after rain; cattle may be encountered.

  • River Ash crossed six times along the route
  • Follows the trackbed of the former Buntingford Branch line
  • Refreshment stop at The Chequers Inn, Wareside
  • Wide open countryside views above the Ash Valley
  • GPX/KML download available from Hertfordshire Walker blog

Walk 4

Widford & Cold Christmas Grand Circuit

Moderate 9.5 miles · 15.4 km ~5 hours 🐕 Dog Friendly
Start: Widford Church (St John the Baptist), SG12 8RL  ·  51.8221°N, 0.0473°E

A rewarding half-day circuit following the Hertfordshire Way from Widford's medieval church, crossing the River Ash into Wareside and on through Babbs Green. Traverse ancient Buckney Wood, reach the tiny lost hamlet of Cold Christmas, cross the River Rib, then return via the Harcamlow Way. Crosses the Greenwich Meridian — twice. Park in lay-bys west of Widford church on the B1004.

  • Widford's 12th–15th century church — a beautiful starting point
  • Crosses the Greenwich Meridian (0° longitude) twice
  • Cold Christmas — a tiny, ancient hamlet with a curious name
  • Follows both the Hertfordshire Way and the Harcamlow Way
  • Rivers Ash and Rib both feature; 600 ft (182 m) total ascent

Hertford Museum · Ref. HETFM2835.101

Timber Cottages, Public House & Barn

A rare Edwardian photograph capturing the heart of Wareside village at the turn of the 20th century. Timber-framed and brick cottages line the lane beside the village pub and an agricultural barn — a scene that speaks directly to Wareside's identity as a working rural community providing grain for Hertfordshire's famous maltings and gravel for the county's expanding road network. The image sits in the Hertford Museum's Beyond Hertford collection, catalogued under both Homes, Pubs and Shops — a reminder of how closely these functions were intertwined in a self-sufficient Victorian and Edwardian village.

Local Legend

The Wareside Treaclemine

No account of Wareside would be complete without mentioning its most celebrated legend. The village is famous throughout Hertfordshire for the mythical Wareside Treaclemine — a playful piece of folklore about a make-believe mine beneath the village from which locals supposedly dug up treacle. Whether it began as a joke at the expense of credulous travellers, or a knowing wink to outsiders, the legend has stuck for generations and remains a charming part of the village's identity. The Francis Frith notes confirm it: locals still know Wareside simply as "the Treacle Mines."

"Wareside is known by locals as the Treacle Mines! ...At the end of the 19th century, Wareside boasted seven public houses."

— Francis Frith Collection, notes to Ref. W290006, c.1955

Practical Information

Getting to Wareside

🚂

By Train

Ware railway station on the Hertford East Branch Line is approximately 2.5 miles away. Trains run from London Liverpool Street via Hertford East.

🚗

By Car

The B1004 linking Ware to Bishop's Stortford passes directly through the village. The A10 can be joined at Thundridge, 2 miles north. Fanhams Hall Road also connects back to Ware.

🚶

On Foot

The Ash Valley Walk connects Wareside to Ware, Widford, and Hunsdon. Footpaths from both village pubs offer circular walks of 3–8 miles through the Hertfordshire countryside.

🚌

By Bus

Limited local bus services connect Wareside to Ware and Hertford. Check Hertfordshire County Council's bus network for current timetables.
